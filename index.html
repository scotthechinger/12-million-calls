What is Safe First Response?

In cities and towns across the country, trained responders are handling 911 calls that do not require police. These unarmed safety responders address concerns involving mental health, substance use, disputes, and welfare checks — resolving issues with care, reducing harm, and improving public safety.

On Safety

Unarmed response dramatically reduces escalation

Across programs nationwide, unarmed responders resolve the vast majority of calls without force, arrest, or injury. By arriving without weapons and prioritizing communication, de-escalation, and clinical assessment, responders reduce fear and volatility at the outset of an interaction. This approach lowers the risk of harm for community members, responders, and law enforcement alike — and helps prevent situations from escalating into crises that require police involvement.

On Professionalism

Responders are trained professionals

AFR responders are trained, certified, and dispatched directly from 911. They bring clinical expertise, lived experience, trauma-informed skills, and cultural knowledge. Many programs use marked vehicles and uniforms. When they arrive, people know that help — real, professional help — has come.

On Cost

Safe First Response saves public dollars

The cost per response is significantly lower than a traditional police response to the same type of call. Programs reduce strain on emergency rooms, police departments, and the court system. Studies of programs like Denver's STAR show meaningful cost savings per call — producing measurable returns across the entire public safety ecosystem.

On Universality

These models work in cities and rural communities

Every community faces the same underlying concerns: untreated mental health needs, interpersonal disputes, welfare checks, and more. Programs are already operating in small towns and rural counties. Some use phone or virtual response where rapid in-person response isn't feasible. The model is tailored to local context — the need is universal.

On Public Safety

This strengthens — not weakens — public safety

Safe First Response reduces inappropriate call volume so that police can focus on situations that genuinely require them. Law enforcement officers across the country report that these programs make their jobs easier, safer, and more effective. This isn't a competition. It's a collaboration — a smarter, fuller approach to keeping communities safe.

National Patterns

What patterns emerge when millions of calls are handled differently?

Programs are launching across the country — in small towns and major cities. The data shows what's possible when we match response to need.

How it works

How Safe First Response Works

When someone needs help, what happens next matters enormously. Safe First Response is built on a simple idea: get the right person to every call, with the right skills, for the right outcome. Here's what that looks like — from the first 911 call to the last follow-up.

01
Before anyone is dispatched

Pre-Response Diversion

Many concerns can be resolved before a responder ever leaves the station.

Not every call to 911 needs an in-person response. A well-designed system can resolve many concerns immediately — by phone, online, or through triage.

Online reporting for non-urgent concerns — noise, minor incidents, follow-ups.
Emergency clinical support over the phone for people in distress.
Mandatory alarm verification before anyone is dispatched.
Medical director oversight for mental health and substance use triage.

A concern, not a crime

Someone needs help. Before anything else can go right, this distinction has to be made — by the dispatcher, the responder, and the system itself.

Setting the scene

Across America, 240 million calls flow into 911 every year. A significant share — mental health welfare checks, disputes between neighbors, people in distress on the street — don't require a police response. They require a human one.

The moment a call is classified as a concern rather than a crime, everything changes. The triage process, the dispatch decision, the person who shows up at the door — all of it turns on this first, critical frame.

What research tells us

Studies of 911 call logs consistently find that 20–40% of police calls involve no criminal element at all. Mental health, substance use, welfare checks, and conflict resolution together constitute a substantial and measurable fraction of emergency calls — calls that trained, unarmed responders handle more safely and at lower cost.

The problem isn't the calls. The problem is the default response.

"Most of what we respond to, nobody needed police. They needed somebody."
— CAHOOTS Responder, Eugene, OR

CAHOOTS — Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets — has been operating in Eugene, Oregon since 1989. In more than three decades, their mobile crisis intervention teams have handled over 17% of Eugene's 911 call volume. They go without police to calls involving mental health concerns, welfare checks, intoxicated individuals, and more.

Their insight — that most calls simply don't require an armed response — is now backed by national data, replicated across dozens of cities, and supported by law enforcement partners who see firsthand what it looks like when the right responder shows up.

Triage at 911

The dispatcher's decision — made in seconds — determines who arrives. It is the invisible architecture of the entire system.

The protocol

When a call comes in, a trained dispatcher runs through a rapid assessment: Is anyone harmed? Is a weapon present? Does this situation require someone with law enforcement authority — or someone with clinical and de-escalation skills?

When the answer is the latter — and it often is — an AFR responder is dispatched directly from 911. The caller hears: "Help is on the way." They don't always know who's coming. But the right person is.

Designing the system

In Denver, STAR — Support Team Assisted Response — developed its triage protocol in partnership with 911 dispatch, the police department, and mental health clinicians. The protocol was tested, refined, and operationalized over months before a single call was routed.

That groundwork is what allows a dispatcher to make the call in real time, with confidence — knowing the team they're sending is equipped for exactly this.

"We trained side by side with dispatch. They know us, they trust us. That trust is the whole system."
— STAR Program Coordinator, Denver, CO

The triage protocol isn't just a checklist — it's a relationship. Dispatchers who work alongside AFR teams develop pattern recognition over time, gaining comfort and confidence in routing calls away from police. Programs that invest in this relationship see smoother, faster, more accurate routing.

Denver's STAR program handles thousands of calls per year, all routed through 911 dispatch. Zero police backup has ever been needed on a STAR call due to safety concerns.

The first two minutes

The responder reads the scene before they've said a word. Everything that comes next depends on what they see — and how they choose to enter.

What the responder sees

The van pulls up slowly. The responder steps out without urgency, without tactical gear, without a weapon on their hip. Their uniform communicates care, not force. Before they've said anything, their arrival signals: this is not a confrontation.

They scan the environment — looking for safety information, emotional cues, context clues about what happened and who is involved. In two minutes of careful observation, an experienced responder builds a working picture of the entire situation.

The science of first contact

Research on crisis response consistently shows that the first moments of contact are the highest-leverage point in any interaction. The emotional state of the person in distress will often mirror the energy of the responder who arrives. Calm, slow, non-threatening entry produces measurably better outcomes — faster de-escalation, greater cooperation, less fear.

AFR responders are trained to understand this — and to use it.

"I park half a block away sometimes. I want them to see me coming. I want them to see that I'm not rushing."
— Crisis Responder, HEART Program, Durham, NC

Durham's HEART — Holistic Empathetic Assistance Response Team — trains responders in what they call "the slow approach": deliberate, visible, unhurried entry that allows the person in distress to process who is arriving and why.

It sounds counterintuitive in an emergency context. But the data from HEART and programs like it shows that slowing down at the front end dramatically speeds up resolution overall — and produces far better outcomes for the people involved.

The conversation that changes everything

The responder introduces themselves and begins building rapport. They are not there to establish compliance. They are there to help.

What engagement looks like

The first words matter. Responders are trained to introduce themselves clearly, use a calm and even voice, maintain open body language, and — critically — to ask rather than tell. "What's going on?" rather than "What happened." "How are you feeling?" rather than "Calm down."

These aren't just soft skills. They are the difference between a conversation that opens and one that closes down. Between a situation that resolves and one that escalates.

Trust as infrastructure

In communities where police responses have historically been traumatic, the arrival of an AFR responder signals something different. Over time, repeat callers learn who to expect — and what to expect from them. That accumulated trust becomes a resource that the entire system draws on.

Engagement isn't just what happens on this call. It is the deposit in a relationship that pays dividends on the next one.

"I just sit with them. Sometimes for a long time. That's the work."
— Peer Specialist, CAHOOTS, Eugene, OR

Peer specialists bring something no clinical credential can fully replicate: lived experience. When a responder can say, genuinely, "I've been where you are" — the dynamic of the conversation changes. Trust forms faster. People open up more. The engagement is real.

CAHOOTS teams typically pair a crisis worker with a medic or counselor. The combination — clinical skill plus human relatability — is a model that many newer programs have adopted and refined.

De-escalation is a clinical skill

Not a personality trait. Not a soft skill. A trained, validated methodology with measurable outcomes. This is what AFR responders do.

What de-escalation actually is

De-escalation is a set of evidence-based verbal and behavioral techniques designed to reduce agitation, fear, and conflict in acute situations. It draws on crisis intervention theory, motivational interviewing, trauma-informed care, and mental health first aid.

AFR responders are trained in validated frameworks — some for dozens of hours before their first call. These aren't soft skills. They are clinical methodologies, tested in research, refined in practice, and applied under real-world pressure every day.

The outcomes data

Every major study of AFR programs finds the same thing: when trained de-escalation specialists respond to mental health and substance-use calls, the rate of use of force drops to near zero. Arrests are rare. Injuries are rarer.

This isn't because AFR calls are easier. It's because the skills work. The same call, the same person, the same level of distress — different responder, dramatically different outcome.

"We don't wing it. Every technique I use, I was trained in. I practiced it. I know why it works."
— Crisis Clinician, ECCO Program, Austin, TX

Austin's ECCO — Emergency Community Care Outreach — trains crisis clinicians in Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality (CAMS), applied suicide intervention skills, motivational interviewing, and psychosis response. The clinical foundation isn't incidental to the model — it is the model.

ECCO clinicians carry no weapons, wear no body armor. Their tools are conversation, empathy, and years of clinical training. On the calls they respond to, those tools are exactly right for the job.

What does this person actually need?

Once calm is established, the real work begins: understanding not just what happened, but why — and what it will take to prevent it from happening again.

Beyond the immediate call

A welfare check about an elderly neighbor might reveal unmanaged diabetes, social isolation, and a lapsed prescription. A dispute between roommates might be rooted in housing insecurity and untreated trauma. A person in distress might be navigating a psychotic episode with no case manager, no medication, and no one who knows their name.

The responder asks: What does this person actually need right now? And in the longer term? The answers point toward a very different kind of response than a call log can capture.

Whole-person assessment

AFR responders are trained to conduct rapid whole-person assessments: physical health, mental health, substance use, social support, housing stability, safety concerns. The goal isn't diagnosis — it's a clear enough picture to connect this person to the right resources at the right level of care.

This is where the difference between a response and a resolution becomes concrete. The assessment determines what's possible. And what's possible is often more than anyone expected.

"The call said welfare check. What I found was a man who hadn't eaten in three days, whose lights had been cut off, and who hadn't talked to anyone in a week. That's not a welfare check. That's a system failing someone."
— Outreach Specialist, Safe Streets, Minneapolis, MN

Minneapolis's Safe Streets program places outreach workers with lived experience of housing instability and mental health challenges alongside clinical professionals. The combination allows for both rigorous clinical assessment and the kind of human-level connection that makes people willing to accept help.

The assessment phase is often the longest part of a Safe Streets response. That time is the point. It's what makes resolution meaningful — and durable.

The concern is addressed.
The person is supported.

This is what success looks like. Not a log entry. Not a cleared call. A person who received the help they needed — from the right person, at the right time.

What resolution means

Resolution doesn't mean the same thing on every call. Sometimes it's a conversation that ends with both parties laughing and shaking hands. Sometimes it's arranging a ride to a crisis stabilization center and sitting with someone until they're ready to go. Sometimes it's connecting a person to a case manager who changes the arc of their life over the following year.

What resolution always means: the immediate concern is addressed, the person is supported, and the call is closed with care — not with a log entry and a cleared radio.

The evidence on outcomes

National data on AFR programs consistently shows high on-scene resolution rates — often 85–95% of calls resolved without referral, transport, or police involvement. But the number that matters most is harder to measure: how many people, after a Safe First Response, called 911 less, accessed services more, and felt — for perhaps the first time — that the system showed up for them.

That story is why this archive exists.

"The call is closed, but I still think about him. I followed up. He got housed. That's why I do this work."
— Crisis Responder, STAR Program, Denver, CO

Across every program featured in this archive, responders describe the same experience: the call that stayed with them. The one where the outcome wasn't just resolution — it was transformation. A person who became a neighbor again. A family that stabilized. A moment of genuine human connection in the middle of a crisis.

These stories are the evidence that numbers alone cannot carry. They are the reason this archive exists, and the reason Safe First Response must scale.

About this project

About This Project

The 12 Million Calls Initiative is a storytelling and advocacy project built to accelerate the adoption of Safe First Response programs nationwide — by making the case clearly, compellingly, and in the words of the people closest to the work.

Why this moment

Why This Moment Matters

Programs that send trained, unarmed professionals to respond to non-violent concerns are working across the country. When people hear about them, they broadly support them. The research is clear. The results are documented.

And yet Safe First Response remains vastly underfunded — viewed by too many decision-makers as an interesting idea rather than a proven public safety solution.

The gap isn't evidence. The gap is awareness, imagination, and political will. This project exists to close that gap.

Project stewardship

Who Is Leading This Work

The Policing Project at NYU School of Law
The Policing Project works to make policing more democratic, equitable, and effective — through research, policy, and community engagement. Its national review of Alternative Response Programs is the most comprehensive assessment of AFR effectiveness to date. The Policing Project leads this initiative, stewards its research infrastructure, and connects programs, policymakers, and communities across the country.
How we work

How We Tell This Story

This project is built on a core belief: the most powerful advocates for Safe First Response are the people doing the work.

That's why the center of this initiative is a growing archive of first-person video — responders, program leaders, community partners, and law enforcement allies speaking in their own words.

We pair those stories with rigorous research, accessible data, and a clear policy framework. Because proof matters — and decision-makers need both the human story and the evidence to act.

Our values

How We Work

We center the voices of the people closest to the work.
We prioritize informed consent, dignity, and agency in all storytelling.
We use language carefully — avoiding frames that distort or diminish this work.
We present evidence honestly, including what programs are still working to improve.
We build tools designed to last — not just to launch.
